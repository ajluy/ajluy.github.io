Most people don't think about how a crane operator actually gets into their cabin. It turns out that for many cranes, the climb can be pretty dangerous. As part of a team project sponsored by Marmon, my specific contributions centered on two areas: prototyping our access solutions and estimating the manufacturing costs of our final design. Our team's solution earned 1st runner up at the Innovate-o-thon competition.

### The Challenge: A Dangerous Climb
The current ladder systems on cranes like the LTM 1060-3.1 have several major flaws. Operators face hazards like ice and slipping in adverse weather, and because ground levels vary and cranes come in different heights, a fixed ladder rarely fits the situation perfectly. These problems are expensive, as falls from crane decks cause serious injuries and significant costs for companies. While the team collectively shaped the design direction, my work focused on getting the core concept into a form that could be communicated and evaluated.

### Prototyping the Adjustable Ladder
Within the team's broader design effort, I worked on producing a physical concept prototype of the adjustable ladder mechanism. Given that this was a 24-hour competition, the goal wasn't comprehensive testing but rather getting something tangible in hand that could communicate the core idea of height adjustability.

I modeled and 3D printed a small-scale version of the telescoping sections to illustrate how the mechanism might work in practice. Even as a concept model, having something physical helped the team think through the geometry and discuss potential issues with the locking behavior in a way that was harder to do from sketches alone.

### Prototyping the Foldable Stair System
I also worked on a physical concept prototype of the foldable stair configuration, which was the team's main departure from a traditional vertical ladder. Again, within the constraints of a 24-hour timeline, the aim was to produce something that could convey the folding concept and spark useful discussion rather than serve as a fully validated design.

The printed model helped illustrate key features the team had converged on: wider step widths for more stable footing, the general folding geometry, and how the system might stow when not in use. It gave us something concrete to reference when explaining the design to judges.

![](/assets/images/ladder/ladder_folded.jpg)

### Estimating Machine Shop Manufacturing Costs
While teammates focused on other aspects of the design and presentation, I worked on estimating what it might cost to manufacture the ladder if it were produced in a professional machine shop setting. This meant breaking the design down into its rough components, including telescoping steel pipe sections, hinge assemblies, serrated safety grating treads, mounting brackets, and hardware, and pricing each against general shop rates.

I looked into material costs for powder-coated steel pipe and safety grating, then applied rough machine shop labor rates for operations like cutting, welding, drilling, and finishing. The goal was a reasonable ballpark estimate to support the team's pitch, giving judges a sense of whether the concept could be viable to produce.

![](/assets/images/ladder/ladder_cost.png)

### Takeaways
Working on the prototyping and cost estimation in a compressed 24-hour window was a good exercise in prioritizing what actually needed to be figured out versus what could be reasonably assumed. Having physical models, even simple printed ones, made a real difference in how the team talked about and refined the concept before presenting it.
