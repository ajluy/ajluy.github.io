As a project lead for the ND Data Club, I helped oversee our team's analysis of a large supermarket shopper dataset, coordinating with company contacts and guiding the team from initial hypotheses through to final recommendations.

The Data

We worked with a full year of transactional data across 2,400+ frequent shopper households. Before the team began digging in, I used my marketing background to frame some early hypotheses around discovery and bundling gaps as likely drivers of underperformance, giving the team a concrete starting point rather than an open-ended search.

![](/assets/images/dataclub/data_metrics.png)

I managed communication with our stakeholders, translating business questions into analytical tasks for the team. Internally, I ran check-ins, assigned category ownership across team members, and pushed our findings toward concrete, business-facing recommendations.

What We Found
After flagging several underperforming categories, including frozen juices, specialty teas, pancake mixes, and pet care supplies, each team member took ownership of a deep-dive. Key findings included:

![](/assets/images/dataclub/data_overview.png)

Teas: Purchases were largely planned and habit-driven. We recommended in-store sampling to broaden trial among existing shoppers.

Pancake Mix & Syrup: Strong co-purchase signals pointed to a bundling opportunity. We suggested linked promotions and shelf placement to capitalize on the natural pairing.

Pet Care Supplies: Low category awareness was the core issue. Co-merchandising with high-traffic items like dog food was our recommended fix.

Takeaway
The project reinforced how useful it is to pair a clear behavioral hypothesis with the data before diving in. Framing the right questions early made our analysis more focused and our final recommendations more actionable for the supermarket chain.
